🧭 PR Orientation — My Subjective Take

This is not official documentation. The official contributing guide is at CONTRIBUTING.md. Read that first.

What follows is my subjective read on how the PR process actually works in practice — not rules, just patterns I've observed as a contributor who cares about the project's health. I'm rainsupreme, a full-time engineer who works on Valkey. These are the opinions of myself (and my AI), not the project's or my employer's.

🌱 First contributions

The project is welcoming but bandwidth-constrained. Your first PR might take longer than you expect. That's not a signal about the quality of your work.

Join the Slack

The Valkey community Slack is a great way to get to know people, find potential reviewers, and get a feel for what's being worked on. It's much easier to get reviewer attention when people know your name from conversation.

Introduce yourself through reviews and discussion

You don't have to open a PR to contribute. Reviewing other people's code and participating in issue discussions is a great way to build familiarity with the codebase and become a known face. When you do open your own PR later, people already know you.

Start small and real

A good first PR: fix a real bug (not a typo), keep it under 100 lines, include a test. Show you understand the codebase conventions before proposing bigger changes. The good first issue label is worth looking at for ideas.

Run Unit tests, integration tests, and clang-format locally first.

This will cover most of the CI job content, and you'll get feedback faster than waiting for queued CI jobs.

Use Draft PR until CI is green

Open your PR as a Draft until CI passes. This signals to reviewers that you're still working on it and saves them from reviewing something that might change. Once green, mark it ready for review.

Be aware: some tests are flaky and may fail for reasons unrelated to your change. In general it is the PR author's responsibility to verify that CI job failures are not caused by the PR changes. Check my unofficial daily test dashboard to see what's been flaky recently.

🔍 Finding the right reviewer

There's no formal reviewer assignment for most PRs. Maintainers pick up what they have context on. If your PR sits 3+ weeks with no activity, a polite ping is appropriate.

The Slack channel

Joining the Valkey community Slack is one of the best ways to find reviewers. You can ask directly who has context on a particular subsystem, and people are generally helpful.

Check the issue

If your PR fixes an issue, the issue discussion often has people who are interested in the problem and likely to review a fix. Tag the issue in your PR with #NNNN so it shows up in the issue's timeline — this naturally surfaces your PR to interested people.

Check git log

git log --follow -n 20 -- path/to/file.c shows who recently touched the files you changed. Cross-reference that with who's been active recently (reviewing, commenting on other PRs).

Don't tag inactive people

Don't tag someone who committed to that file 2 years ago and hasn't been seen since. Active reviewers are worth more than domain experts who've moved on.

✅ What makes a PR land quickly

Keep it small and focused

One thing per PR. Reviewers are busy; a 50-line fix gets reviewed in days, a 2000-line refactor sits for months. If you have a big change, ask yourself: can I split this into a preparatory refactor + the actual change? Usually yes, and both halves move faster as separate PRs.

Write a clear description

Answer three questions: what changes, why this change is correct, and how to verify it. Don't make the reviewer reverse-engineer your intent. A good PR description is a gift to the reviewer.

Tag the issue

If your PR fixes or relates to an existing issue, always reference it with Fixes #NNNN or Relates to #NNNN in the PR description. This links the PR to the issue, shows you did your homework, and helps potential reviewers discover your PR from the issue page.

Link to prior context

For anything non-trivial: is there a prior discussion thread? A mailing list post? A design doc? Link it. Context helps reviewers make decisions faster.

⚠️ What makes a PR die quietly

Going quiet after feedback

If a reviewer comments and you don't respond within a few weeks, the PR is effectively dead. The reviewer has moved on. Even a "thanks, working on it" keeps the thread alive. If life got busy, a brief "I'll get back to this" is better than silence.

Scope creep mid-review

If a reviewer asks for a small fix and you come back with three unrelated cleanup commits, they have to re-review from scratch. Make the requested changes and nothing else. File a follow-up PR for the other things you noticed.

Duplicating an existing open PR

Search before submitting. gh search prs --repo valkey-io/valkey --state open "your keywords" takes 30 seconds. The zrangebyscore bug had 3 open PRs simultaneously — none of them got extra attention for it, they just fragmented reviewer energy.

🟡 Understanding major-decision-pending

The major-decision-pending label means the community hasn't reached consensus on whether to take the feature at all. It's not a blocker on your end — your code is fine. It means the project is still debating the direction.

These can take weeks to months to resolve. Currently 38 open PRs carry this label, some for over a year. Patience is the primary tool. Pinging maintainers won't speed up the decision — the decision process has its own timeline.

What can help: if there's a public discussion thread, contribute to it thoughtfully. Real-world use cases and data are more persuasive than enthusiasm. If there's no thread, starting one in GitHub Discussions can sometimes unblock things.
